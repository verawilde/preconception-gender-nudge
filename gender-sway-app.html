Exploring what the (messy) science suggests about factors that may influence baby sex

This tool lets people explore the messy scientific evidence on possible sex ratio shifts. Effect sizes are small, perhaps on the order of 5-15 percentage points at best, if there is a real signal in the noise at all (we don't know).

Mechanisms are unknown: it could be differential fertilization (which sperm wins, or which the egg lets in) or differential viability (which pregnancy continues). We're aiming for net effects that result in a baby of your preferred gender.

üêª Find the bear: A note on epistemic humility

Sex ratios vary naturally by only a few percentage points. Even if these associations are real (and that's a big if), the effects are subtle. No intervention can guarantee a specific outcome.

This tool is for exploration and education. The research cited is observational, not experimental. We don't know if these factors cause sex ratio shifts or just happen to be correlated with them. We don't know if attempting to manipulate them would work, or what unintended consequences might follow.

As with cancer screenings and vaccine endpoints, we have to think carefully about what we're measuring, what we want to know, and how much uncertainty remains. The endpoint rainbow here is wide: fertilization, implantation, viability, birth, and all the biological and evolutionary mechanisms in between.

Count the bodies. Here, the bodies we're counting are actual bodies: babies. This is a beautiful case study where we can empirically test whether dietary and lifestyle interventions shift sex ratios compared to population baselines. With enough participants tracking their efforts and outcomes, and follow-up studies investigating mechanisms further, we could eventually move from correlation to causation‚Äîif there's a signal in the noise.
